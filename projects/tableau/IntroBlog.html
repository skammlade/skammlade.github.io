The Changing Global Diet: Intro Blog

Across the globe, national diets have become more diverse

Diets are comprised of more unique crop products and are less dominated by one particular crop. The number of crops that comprise the global average diet has increased 30% from 27 crops to 36 crops between 1961-2009. In addition, the most dominant foods in a diet have become less important in contributing calories.

Take for example, the Bangladesh diet. This is the least diverse diet on the planet and is comprised of 27 crops, with 70% of the calories coming from rice alone. Conversely, Cameroon has the most diverse diet comprised of 36 unique crops each contributing more equally to the diet.

However, this increase in diet diversity often comes at the sacrifice of native foods for introduced foods

Oilcrops such as soybean, palm oil, and sunflowers have surged in their contribution to global diets. Other staples such as cassava, millets, sorghum and yam have become marginalized.

Often native staples are being substituted by non-native globalized staples.

As crops are shared, diets across the globe have become more similar

National diets are more similar in their composition.

But, more propserous countries have better access to diverse diets

A greater GDP correlates with a diet comprised of more uniqe crops that contribute more evenly to the diet.

Social and political turmoil can also negatively influence a country's access to diverse diet
