The Changing Global Diet: Intro Blog

Across the globe, national diets have become more diverse

Diversity was measured in a number of ways - the number of unique crops species and the proportion with which each species is represented in the food supply. A diet is most diverse when it contains a number of unique crops and each crop has a more evenly distributed contribution to the total food supply. FAO tracks a limited number of crops so this has a maximum of 53 commodities including animal products.

Diets are now comprised of more unique crop products and are less dominated by one particular crop. The number of crops that contribute to the global average diet has increased 30% from 27 crops to 36 crops between 1961-2009. In addition, the most dominant foods in a diet have become less important in contributing calories. Major staples have undergone dramatic diversification with many countries no longer relying on one major staple, but two or three major staples.

Take for example, the Bangladesh diet. This is the least diverse diet on the planet and is comprised of 27 crops, with 70% of the calories coming from rice alone. Conversely, Cameroon has the most diverse diet comprised of 36 unique crops each contributing more equally to the diet. Of the 154 countries observed, United Arab Emirates has seen the most dramatic increase in number of corps contributing to their food supplies across the time period. In 1961 their diet was comprised of only nine crops and 42 in 2009, a 370% increase.

In general, prosperous countries have better access to diverse diets, but with some exceptions

A greater GDP correlates with a diet comprised of more uniqe crops that contribute more evenly to the diet.

The increase in dietary diversity often comes at the sacrifice of native/local/traditional staples, which become more marginalized

Oilcrops such as soybean, palm oil, and sunflowers have surged in their contribution to global diets. Other staples such as cassava, millets, sorghum and yam have become marginalized.

Often native staples are being substituted by non-native globalized staples.

As crops are shared, diets across the globe have become more similar

National diets are more similar in their composition.

Social and political turmoil can also negatively influence a country's access to diverse diet
