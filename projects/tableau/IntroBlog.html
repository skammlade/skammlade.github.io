Here’s five surprising ways people’s diets have changed over the past 50 years

By Sara Kammlade and Colin K. Khoury

It'’s always interesting, albeit disconcerting, to learn that one’s preconceptions are largely misconceptions. This sums up the experience of our team of biodiversity researchers, who dived into global data on what plants people have eaten around the world over the past 50 years. We were looking for evidence of the gradual disappearance of the variety of crops in people’s diets, an assumption aligned with the oft-repeated mantra that we have already lost 75% of our agricultural diversity. We didn’t find it, for reasons explained more fully .

What we did find was change, change, change. Massive change in the foods people eat. Crop winners and losers. And most of all, evidence of much greater similarity in diets worldwide, as economic and agricultural development has proceeded and as food systems have globalized.

When we published our findings of increasing homogeneity in global food supplies, we hadn’t yet found a good way to make the underlying data readily visible to interested readers. This is why we are tremendously excited to announce the publication of our new Changing Global Diet website, which provides interactive explorations for 152 countries over five decades of change. We hope that you enjoy your investigations through time, and are as surprised as we were with the evident trends.
